Data Sources Used by RadarOmega

Radar-Based Products
RadarOmega leverages high-resolution, single-site radar data from around the world, offering reflectivity, velocity, and dual-polarization radar products. 
radaromega.com

Official Weather Alerts & Outlooks
The app integrates a wide range of real-time and forecast products from trusted agencies, including:

Storm Prediction Center (SPC) — convective outlooks, mesoscale discussions

National Hurricane Center (NHC) — tropical outlooks, hurricane hunter data

Weather Prediction Center (WPC) — excessive rainfall outlooks, surface analysis

National Weather Service (NWS) — tornado, severe thunderstorm, flash flood warnings, and other alerts

Fire weather outlooks, winter weather forecasts, drought monitor, CPC temperature/precipitation outlooks, NEXRAD hail history, power outage overlays, METARs, spotter network, and more 
radaromega.com

Lightning Detection
RadarOmega features lightning detection and strike data (with animation), giving users insight into electrical storm activity

Satellite, MRMS & Model Data (Subscription Tiers)
Upgrading to subscription tiers (Alpha, Beta, Gamma) unlocks access to:

Satellite imagery

MRMS (Multi-Radar / Multi-Sensor) data—hail, rotation, lightning, precipitation products

Numerical forecast models—HRRR, NAM (3km & 12km), RAP, GFS, ECMWF, HWRF, HMON

Model overlays, advanced animation capabilities (up to 250 frames), 3D radar/satellite visualization, national digital forecast database, volumetric views, long-range radar history, storm report archives, custom palettes, and more 
radaromega.com

Apple WeatherKit (General Weather Data)
Especially on iOS, general “current weather” and forecast info (outside of radar and alerts) are supplied via Apple’s WeatherKit

TL;DR

RadarOmega pulls data from a wide set of reputable and official sources:

Radar data from individual radar sites worldwide.

Alerts and outlooks from federal agencies like SPC, NWS, NHC, WPC, etc.

Lightning data, satellite feeds, MRMS, and forecast models (in higher-tier subscriptions).

General weather conditions pulled from Apple’s WeatherKit, at least on iOS.
