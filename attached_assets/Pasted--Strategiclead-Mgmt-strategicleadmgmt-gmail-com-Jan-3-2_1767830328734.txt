What’s SOC2
Love this question — because this is one of those “invisible gatekeepers” that decides whether big companies will work with you or not.

I’ll explain SOC 2 in plain English, then map it directly to Storm / Disaster Direct so you know whether and when you need it.

🛡 What is SOC 2?
SOC 2 = System and Organization Controls Type 2

It’s a formal security and compliance audit that proves:

“This company protects customer data properly and has strong internal controls.”

It’s not software.
It’s not a tool.
It’s a third-party certification issued after an independent audit of your systems and processes.

Think of it as:

🧾 A trust badge for enterprise clients.

What SOC 2 proves
SOC 2 measures whether you meet standards in 5 areas:

Principle	Means
Security	You prevent unauthorized access
Availability	Your system stays up and recoverable
Confidentiality	Sensitive data is protected
Processing Integrity	Data is accurate and not corrupted
Privacy	Personal data is handled correctly
Why big companies care
Utilities, insurers, banks, governments, and primes cannot legally share data with vendors that fail basic security requirements.

SOC 2 tells them:

You encrypt data

You control access

You log activity

You can recover from failures

You have policies and procedures

Without it, procurement often stops your deal automatically.

🔹 SOC 2 Type 1 vs Type 2
Type	What it means
Type 1	Your controls exist (point-in-time check)
Type 2	Your controls work over time (3–12 months)
Most enterprises require Type 2.

🧠 Plain-language version
SOC 2 means: “An outside auditor has verified that we run our software and handle data in a safe, controlled, and trustworthy way.”

🧩 What SOC 2 actually looks at
They don’t audit your code quality — they audit how you run your company:

Who has access to what?

Are passwords protected and rotated?

Is data encrypted?

Are changes tracked?

Is there incident response?

Is there backup & recovery?

Is there employee security training?

Are vendors vetted?

Are logs stored?

It’s as much process as it is technology.

⏳ When you actually need SOC 2
You don’t need it yet if:
You’re building MVP

Testing with friendly partners

Pre-revenue or early pilot stage

You do need it when:
Selling to utilities, governments, insurers

Handling sensitive personal or financial data

Being asked to fill out security questionnaires

Procurement asks “Are you SOC 2 compliant?”
