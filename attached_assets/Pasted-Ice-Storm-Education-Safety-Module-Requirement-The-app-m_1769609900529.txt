Ice Storm Education & Safety Module Requirement

The app must include a built-in Ice Storm Education & Safety Module that clearly explains the dangers of ice storms, why trees fail during icing events, and when it is safe to remove storm-damaged trees.

This section is designed to educate homeowners, contractors, and field crews.

1️⃣ Why Ice Storms Are So Dangerous

The app must explain:

Ice accumulation adds extreme weight to trees.

Just ½ inch of ice can add hundreds of pounds of extra load to branches.

Ice coats every surface evenly — leaves, twigs, limbs, and trunks — multiplying total stress.

Combined with wind, ice becomes exponentially more dangerous.

Frozen trees become rigid and lose flexibility, making them more likely to snap.

Ice storms often bring power line hazards, hidden structural damage, and delayed failures (trees that fall hours or days later).

The module should explain this in simple, easy-to-understand language for the public and in more technical language for contractors.

2️⃣ Why Trees “Explode” or Snap During Ice Storms

The app must explain the science behind tree failures:

Causes of Tree Explosions or Splitting:

Rapid weight overload from ice accumulation

Internal moisture expansion when freezing

Brittle wood under freezing temperatures

Wind torque stress

Weak branch unions (V-shaped crotches)

Existing decay or hollow trunks

Sudden stress release when limbs break

When limbs snap under heavy ice, stored tension can cause:

Violent splitting

Sudden limb ejection

“Exploding” trunk failures

Shattered branch scatter

The app should clarify that trees do not literally explode from ice — they fail due to mechanical stress and stored energy release.

3️⃣ Why Trees Fall on Structures During Ice Events

The module must explain contributing factors:

Excess canopy weight

Saturated soil causing root plate failure

Shallow root systems

Wind direction combined with ice load

Structural imbalance in the canopy

Pre-existing root damage or decay

Include explanation of:

Root ball lift

Uprooting vs. trunk shear

Limb drop vs. whole tree failure

4️⃣ When Is It Safe to Remove Ice-Damaged Trees?

The app must clearly explain:

It is NOT safe to remove trees when:

Ice is still actively falling

Limbs are still under load

Power lines are involved

The structure is unstable

Wind conditions are ongoing

Secondary collapse risk exists

It may be safer when:

Ice has melted or shed naturally

Power has been confirmed disconnected

The tree has stabilized

A qualified professional has assessed the site

The app must strongly emphasize:
⚠️ Ice-damaged trees are high-risk removals and require trained professionals.

5️⃣ What Is Required to Remove Ice-Damaged Trees on Structures

The app must educate on proper requirements:

Required:

Certified arborist or trained storm crew

Proper PPE (helmets, eye protection, cut-resistant gear)

Aerial lift or crane when needed

Rigging systems

Chainsaws rated for hardwood cutting

Traffic control if roadside

Utility coordination if lines are involved

Structural assessment of damaged building

High-Risk Conditions Include:

Tensioned limbs (“widowmakers”)

Compressed limbs pinned under load

Split trunks under pressure

Trees entangled in power lines

Frozen wood that may shatter unpredictably

6️⃣ Risk Warnings for Homeowners

The app must educate homeowners that:

Cutting a limb under tension can cause violent snapback.

Ice-damaged trees may fail without warning.

Climbing iced trees is extremely dangerous.

Insurance often requires professional mitigation.

Encourage:

Stay clear of impacted areas.

Do not attempt DIY removal.

Document damage with photos.

Contact licensed professionals.

7️⃣ Visual & Educational Features

The app should include:

Diagrams showing tension vs. compression zones

Illustrations of root failure vs trunk failure

Ice load weight charts

Before/after examples

Safety checklists

Storm risk level indicators
